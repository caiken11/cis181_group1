The Pizza Debate – Deep Dish vs. Tavern Style

Overview

While deep-dish is the world-famous postcard of Chicago, tavern-style is the city’s true daily bread. This storied rivalry isn't just about thickness; it’s a battle between a culinary “main event” and a social “bar snack.”

At a Glance: The Rivalry

Feature Deep-Dish Tavern-Style
Crust Type Thick, buttery, biscuit-like Ultra-thin, cracker-like
Cut Style Standard wedges "Party Cut" (squares)
Best Eaten With a knife and fork With one hand (and a beer)
Vibe Special occasion / Tourist favorite Neighborhood bar / Game day staple
Sausage Large patties or chunks Crumbled or small discs

Deep-Dish: The Knife-and-Fork Giant

The History

Born in 1943 at Pizzeria Uno, deep-dish was created by Ike Sewell and Ric Riccardo to transform pizza from a light appetizer into a filling, stand-alone meal. It reshaped Chicago’s food identity forever.

What Makes It Unique

  • The Build: Constructed “upside down” — cheese on the bottom, toppings in the middle, crushed tomato sauce on top.
  • The Crust: Buttery, flaky, golden, and closer to a savory pie crust than bread.
  • The Experience: A sit-down, knife-and-fork meal that takes time and appetite.

Top Recommended Spots

Top Recommended Spots

  1. Pequod’s Pizza (Lincoln Park): Famous for its caramelized halo of burnt cheese around the edge.
  2. Lou Malnati’s: Known for the Buttercrust and lean, high‑quality sausage patties.
  3. Pizzeria Uno/Due (River North): The historic birthplace of deep‑dish; essential for purists.
  4. Giordano’s: The king of stuffed pizza, featuring a second thin layer of dough under the sauce.

Tavern-Style: Chicago’s Everyday Slice

The History

Emerging in the 1930s and 40s (notably at spots like Vito & Nick’s and Home Run Inn), this style was designed for South Side bars. Owners wanted a snack that encouraged drinking without filling patrons up too quickly—easy to eat with one hand while holding a beer in the other.

What Makes It Unique

  • The "Party Cut": Instead of wedges, tavern-style is cut into small squares. The tiny corner pieces are the most coveted for their extra crunch.
  • The Crust: Rolling pins squeeze the air out of the dough, creating a cracker-thin, ultra-crispy base that never sags under toppings.
  • The Toppings: Spread edge-to-edge, leaving almost no bare crust.

Top Recommended Spots

  1. Vito & Nick’s (Ashburn): The gold standard. Family-owned since 1923; cash only and proudly old-school.
  2. Pizz’amici (West Town): A newer heavyweight (ranked highly in 2025/2026) using fermented sourdough for a modern twist.
  3. Barnaby’s of Northbrook: Famous for its crimped edges and nostalgic, wood‑paneled atmosphere.
  4. Pat’s Pizza (Lincoln Park): Frequently cited by locals as the thinnest, crispest crust in the city.
